Dearly Beloved

Amol's Funeral Party was a living funeral for close friends. A reminder, an RSVP test, a tight door, and then a room full of people dressed in funeral black saying the kind of things they might otherwise save for the worst possible timing.

The rule was simple: if you had been confirmed, you had a scarce spot. If you had not, you hung in there to the bitter end. The service began around the appointed hour, followed by resurrection, drinks, food, dancing, and a general refusal to be overly normal about any of it.

"Tonight's not a eulogy, not really. And nor is it merely a celebration. It is something stranger and more human than either of those things alone."

This page keeps the remains in order: how it worked, what was said, what to copy, and what to avoid unless you also enjoy making your friends wonder whether they have joined a cult, a wake, or an unusually well-run birthday.

How To Throw A Funeral

  1. Send the wrong invitation correctly. "Hey dearly departed" is useful. It tells people this is happening, they are on a tight list, and they should stop pretending RSVP forms are optional literature.
  2. Make the door scarce. A living funeral should not feel like a conference. Confirm people. Make them update their plus-ones. Let the waitlist suffer with dignity.
  3. Dress the room. Funeral black that rules. Stylish, dramatic, church-compatible. The costume helps people understand they are not walking into drinks. They are walking into a scene.
  4. Ask for afterlife gifts. Advice, an offering, a drawing, a prophecy, an accusation, a blessing, a correction, or a lie. Give guests a way to bring something stranger than flowers.
  5. Collect eulogy intentions. If someone plans to say a few words, sing, perform, or otherwise interfere with the solemnity of the proceedings, ask them what they have in mind.
  6. Build the service for whiplash. Invocation, songs, best friends, legal documents, venture jokes, open floor, resurrection. The solemn parts need jokes. The jokes need witnesses.
  7. Keep real-time trouble in one place. Private group chat, last wishes, late location details, emergency corrections. Do not make 150 people text the corpse.
  8. End before reverence curdles. Service, resurrection, drinks, food, dancing. Please come on time. The deceased would have hated that.

Final Arrangements

Arrival

Guests arrived as confirmed mourners, friends, coconspirators, and people who had successfully made it through the scarce door. Dress code: funeral black, but make it rule.

The Service

John Merz opened the proceedings. Matte Chi, Pascale Sarva, Alfred Steiner, Tom Sanford, John Post Lee, Jessa Howe, and Jay Freeman said words, made trouble, and returned Amol to the living with interest. Cecily Sanford supplied the musical evidence.

The Afterlife

After the ceremony: resurrection, drinks, food, dancing, and the temporary home of the party's trouble and delight. Final words were issued. Better news was not available.

Eulogy Intentions

The readable transcript is the primary record. These are the field notes from the proceedings.

  • John Merz: made the room legal in the eyes of chronos, kairos, Rilke, and whoever was worried this might be too weird.
  • Matte Chi: listened, then gave the flowers directly: love, pride, and friendship with no useful exit route.
  • Pascale Sarva: sunscreen, parents, travel, New York, California, knees, old love letters, and other instructions for surviving being alive.
  • Alfred Steiner: limited partners, unicorn bait, philosophy as training for death, and the venture-backed case for making it this long.
  • Tom Sanford: Columbia, art, impossible projects, goldfish bowls, and the best-friend testimony Amol clearly desired and absolutely got.
  • Jessa Howe: a last will and testament for a client who was, for all legally relevant purposes, alive and judging the delivery.
  • Jay Freeman: symbolic lateness, meaningful aliveness, and the certainty that Amol would soon return with a plan, three podcasts, and a redesigned website.
