AMOL'S FUNERAL PARTY

How To Throw A Funeral

A living funeral is a party that steals the best part of a memorial service and gives it back to the person in the room. This page is a working guide, event overview, and archive for Amol's version: speeches, jokes, songs, ritual, practical planning, and a photo gallery coming soon.

Overview

This was not a fake death so much as a useful inversion. The person being remembered was alive, present, and able to hear it. The room borrowed the grammar of a funeral: invocation, eulogies, songs, legal jokes, last words, resurrection energy, and the permission structure that lets people speak plainly.

"Tonight's not a eulogy, not really. And nor is it merely a celebration. It is something stranger and more human than either of those things alone."

The archive here keeps the event legible for people who were there, people who missed it, and anyone reckless enough to host one of their own.

How To Throw A Funeral

  1. Pick the premise clearly. Decide whether this is a living funeral, roast, memorial rehearsal, birthday ritual, or art project. Tell guests the truth plainly enough that they know how to show up.
  2. Choose a room with ritual weight. A church, hall, studio, home, club, or back room can work. The point is not solemnity; the point is a space where attention can gather.
  3. Build a real program. Use an officiant or host, 5-8 prepared speakers, musical interludes, one open-floor section, and a closing gesture. Keep the arc tight enough that the party survives the ceremony.
  4. Invite people to be specific. The best speeches avoid generic admiration. Ask for stories, reversals, debts, arguments, artifacts, jokes, and evidence.
  5. Let comedy and feeling coexist. A living funeral works because it does not flatten the person into virtue. Roasts, legal bits, affectionate insults, and absurd props can make the sincere parts easier to hear.
  6. Give the living guest a role. They can sit silently, enter late, lie in state, answer questions, receive objects, make closing remarks, or simply listen. Choose the role before the night starts.
  7. Document everything. Record audio, collect scripts, photograph the room, save the program, and capture guest notes. The archive becomes part of the gift.
  8. End in motion. Move from ceremony to party: bells, drinks, food, dancing, a toast, or a deliberately unserious transition. Nobody should leave trapped in the coffin of the concept.

Program

Opening Invocation

John Merz opened the room with the frame: chronos, kairos, Rilke, death as a companion, and the permission to say now what usually gets postponed.

Speeches, Songs, and Witness

The evening moved through prepared tributes by Matte Chi, Pascale Sarva, Alfred Steiner, Tom Sanford, John Post Lee, Jessa Howe, and Jay Freeman, with musical interludes by Cecily Sanford.

Closing Benediction

The formal record closes with bells, cracks where the light gets in, and the instruction to continue into the richness and geology of the dance.

Speech Highlights

The readable transcript is the primary record. These notes are a quick map of the terrain.

  • Matte Chi: friendship as a direct address: "I love you. I'm proud of you."
  • Pascale Sarva: advice, sunscreen, parents, travel, New York, California, and the comedy of being young and old at once.
  • Alfred Steiner: a venture-backed eulogy, complete with limited partners, unicorns, reversals, philosophy, and a high tolerance for risk.
  • Tom Sanford: the long friendship, Columbia, art, ambition, and the gift of finally giving flowers in public.
  • Jessa Howe: the last will and testament of a person alive enough to judge the performance.
  • Jay Freeman: the resurrection clause: Amol rising again with a business plan, podcasts, and a redesigned website.
